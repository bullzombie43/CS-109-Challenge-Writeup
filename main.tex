\subsection*{Methods and Scope}
There are dozens of different approaches to create deepfakes, some of which include: Generative Adversial Networks and Encoder-Decoder Networks for aceswaps, mesh/landmark based and end to end models for face reenactment, diffusion models for full face generation and much more. Every method leaves its own form of traces, so it is difficult to generalize a single model to detect all types of deepfakes. For my project I focused on deepfakes of faces specifically, training for and testing on the FaceForensics++\footnote{add their citation} benchmark dataset.

\subsection*{Qualities of a Deepfake}
Deepfaked Videos and images, while difficult to spot with the human eye, often leave traces that computers can pick up on. Often times deepfaked videos, especially those made by Viggle, will overlay two videos on top of each other. If we compare the face region of a video with the background the compression artifacts and color grading may be different. Between frames the color grading of a face is also likely to vary more in a deepfake then with a normal video. For every video I extracted 5 features which should, in theory, differ between deepfaked and normal videos: DCT\footnote{what is DCT} divergence between face and background, block-DCT divergence for compression differences, mean difference between DCT coefficients, frame to frame MAD\footnote{what is MAD} of face crops, and laplacian variance ratio of face and background. This paper is focusing on the techniques used to analyze these features, as such the specifics of the features themselves is not important, it is sufficient to assume that differences in each of these values exists between deepfakes and normal videos. That being said if you are interested in what each feature is, and how I came to use them (I spent a lot of time testing different features) I will include a writeup at the end.

\subsection*{Naive Bayes}
Naive Bayes is a machine learning algorithm for classification which makes the assumption that all features are independent of each other. With any classification\footnote{for all our equations we are assuming that y is binary ie. $y={0,1}$ as a video can only be a deepfake or not a deepfake} algorithm, we seek to choose the value $y$ that maximizes $P(Y = y | X = X)$. 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\hat y &= \text{arg max} \ P(Y = | X = X) \\
&= \text{arg max} \ \frac{P(Y = y)P(X = x | Y = y)}{P(X = x)} \\
&= \text{arg max} \ P(Y = y)P(X = x | Y = y) \text{(since $P(X=x)$ is a constant w.r.t. $Y$}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Naive Bayes makes the incorrect assumption that each characteristic $x$ is independent of one another given y, reducing the space needed from $O(n^2)$ to $O(n)$ if $|x| = n$.
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\hat y &= \text{arg max} \ P(Y = y)P(X = x | Y = y) \\
&= \text{arg max} \ P(Y = y)\prod_i P(X_i = x_i | Y = y) \\
&= \text{arg max} \ \log P(Y = y) + \sum_i \log P(X_i = x_i | Y = y)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Maximum Likelihood Estimation}

With equation 2 we can estimate the probability of a deepfake if we have the probablilities of each feature for both deepfakes and normal videos, but how will we get those? This is where the "training" part of our model is introduced. Essentially for both deepfakes and normal videos we will create a probability distribution of each feature $x_i$. To calculate each $P(X_i = x_i | Y =y)$ we will use the probability mass function of these distributions. To find these distributions, we will employ a technique, Maximum Likelihood Estimation\footnote{MLE makes the assumption that are data is identically distributed}, where we will choose values for our parameters $\theta$ that maximize the likelihood of our data. 

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(X_i = x_i | \Theta = \theta) \\
\hat \theta = \text{arg max} \ L(\theta) 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Because log is a monotone function\footnote{what does this mean}, the argmax of our log likehood it will be equal to the argmax of our likelihood simplifying the math.

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
LL(\theta) &= \log L(\theta) \\
&= \log \prod_{i=1}^n f(X_i = x_i | \Theta = \theta) \\
&= \sum_{i = 1}^n \log f(X_i = x_i | \Theta = \theta)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

To use MLE we must guess at the underlying distribution of our features. MLE is compatible any distribution provided you can define it's PDF and PMF. I started by assuming most my features were normal, however after examining the graphed data I also tried a Gamma\footnote{link to gamma} distribution.

\subsubsection*{Normal MLE Estimation}
A Gaussian has two parameters we need to estimate: $\mu = \theta_0, \sigma^2 = \theta_1 $ 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L(\theta) &= \prod_{i=1}^n f(X_i|\theta) \\
&= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\theta_1}}e^{-\frac{(x_i-\theta_0)^2}{2\theta_1}} \\
LL(\theta) &= \sum_{i=1}^n \left[-\log\sqrt{2\pi\theta_1} - \frac{1}{2\theta_1}(x_i - \theta_0)^2\right] \\
&= -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\theta_1) - \frac{1}{2\theta_1}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \theta_0)^2 \\
\frac{\partial LL(\theta)}{\partial \theta_0} &= \frac{1}{\theta_1}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \theta_0) = 0
\implies \hat{\theta}_0 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i = \bar{x} \\
\frac{\partial LL(\theta)}{\partial \theta_1} &= -\frac{n}{2\theta_1} + \frac{1}{2\theta_1^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \theta_0)^2 = 0
\implies \hat{\theta}_1 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar{x})^2
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Gamma MLE Estimation}
A Gamma has two parameters we need to estimate: $\alpha = \theta_0, \beta  = \theta_1 $ 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L(\theta) &= \prod_{i=1}^n f(X_i|\theta) \\
&= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\Gamma(\theta_0)\theta_1^{\theta_0}} x_i^{\theta_0-1} e^{-x_i/\theta_1} \\
LL(\theta) &= \sum_{i=1}^n \left[-\log\Gamma(\theta_0) - \theta_0\log\theta_1 + (\theta_0-1)\log x_i - \frac{x_i}{\theta_1}\right] \\
&= -n\log\Gamma(\theta_0) - n\theta_0\log\theta_1 + (\theta_0-1)\sum_{i=1}^n\log x_i - \frac{1}{\theta_1}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
There is unfortunately no closed form solution for the Gamma MLE estimation, it must instead be estimated numerically. I used \verb|scipy.stats.gamma.fit|\footnote{Here is a link to how scipy implements it}.

\subsubsection*{Kernel Density Estimation}
If you looked closely at figure 1 earlier, you may have noticed the existence of a third line of fit ``KDE (Scott)'',
which actually matches our data better than both versions of MLE. Kernel Density Estimation
\footnote{https://faculty.washington.edu/yenchic/17Sp\_403/Lec7-density.pdf}, or KDE, is a non-parametric density
estimation technique, meaning unlike MLE it makes no assumption about the underlying distribution of our data. Instead,
KDE constructs a smooth estimate of the PDF by placing a \textit{kernel} --- a small symmetric bump function --- centered
at each data point and summing them together.\footnote{KDE does not change how Naive Bayes works nor it's underlying assumption about independance, it only changes how we estimate the probability of each given feature}

\begin{equation}
\hat{f}_h(x) = \frac{1}{nh} \sum_{i=1}^n K\!\left(\frac{x - x_i}{h}\right)
\end{equation}

where $K$ is the kernel function and $h > 0$ is the \textit{bandwidth}, which controls the smoothness of the estimate.
The most common choice is the Gaussian kernel:

\begin{equation}
K(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}
\end{equation}

The bandwidth $h$ is the key tuning parameter of KDE\footnote{Too small a bandwidth leads to an overfit, noisy estimate;
too large and the estimate is oversmoothed and loses structure}. Scott's rule, which is what we use here, estimates an
optimal bandwidth automatically from the data:

\begin{equation}
h = n^{-1/5} \hat{\sigma}
\end{equation}

where $\hat{\sigma}$ is the standard deviation of the sample. Once we have our KDE estimate $\hat{f}_h(x)$, we use it
directly in place of the parametric PDF from MLE to compute $P(X_i = x_i \mid Y = y)$ for each feature.

While it was outside the scope of CS109, I was curious how KDE would perform compared to the MLE approach for Naive Bayes, as our data does not seem to follow any clean parametric family. I used \verb|sklearn.neighbors.KernelDensity| with \verb|kernel='gaussian'| and \verb|bandwidth='scott'|.

\subsection*{Logistic Regression}
Logistic regression is also a classification algorithm. It makes the central assumption that $P(y|x)$ can be approximated with a sigmoid function applied to a linear combination of input features. 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(Y = 1 | X = x) = \sigma(z) \text{ where } z = \theta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^m \theta_i x_i
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Logistic similarly uses MLE to estimate parameters, however it requires the assumption that $Y \sim Bern(p)$ where $p = \sigma(\theta^Tx)$. 


\subsubsection*{Bernoulli MLE}

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L(\theta) &= \prod_{i=1}^n P(Y = y^{(i)} | X = \mathbf{x}^{(i)}) \\
&= \prod_{i=1}^n \sigma(\theta^T \mathbf{x}^{(i)})^{y^{(i)}} \cdot \left[1 - \sigma(\theta^T \mathbf{x}^{(i)})\right]^{(1-y^{(i)})} \\
LL(\theta) &= \sum_{i=1}^n y^{(i)} \log \sigma(\theta^T \mathbf{x}^{(i)}) + (1 - y^{(i)}) \log\left[1 - \sigma(\theta^T \mathbf{x}^{(i)})\right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

We use the gradient descent algorithm\footnote{link to gradient descent explanation} to estimate parameters($\theta$) that maximize our likelihood.

\subsubsection*{Youden's J Threshold}
If our model produces a probability $P(\text{fake} | X = x)$ that any video is a deepfake, how do we interpret that probability? A naive approach is to classify a video as fake if $P(\text{fake} | X = x) > 0.5$.\footnote{We used an even split of deepfakes and real videos in both training and test, so 0.5 makes sense here. If instead the split was 0.7 and 0.3 we might use a different default.} However our raw probability scores (what MLE or KDE outputs) could be systematically biased towards one side. A common solution to this is to calibrate your model, with a method such as Platt's scaling\footnote{What is platt scaling}. You want to calibrate your model such that it's output probabilities reflect the actual distribution of reality. I was interested in finding a simpler solution, which only adjusted the threshold for classification and stumbled upon Youden's J Threshold. 

Once we have our posterior probability estimates from Bayes' theorem, we must choose a decision threshold $\tau$ such 
that we classify a video as a deepfake if $P(Y = 1 | \mathbf{x}) > \tau$. The naive choice of $\tau = 0.5$ is not 
always optimal, particularly in the presence of class imbalance. Instead, we use Youden's J Statistic 
\footnote{Youden, W.J. (1950). Index for rating diagnostic tests. \textit{Cancer}, 3(1), 32--35.} to select the 
threshold that best balances sensitivity and specificity:

\begin{equation}
J = \text{Sensitivity} + \text{Specificity} - 1 = \text{TPR} - \text{FPR}
\end{equation}

where TPR (True Positive Rate) and FPR (False Positive Rate) are defined as:

\begin{equation}
\text{TPR} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \qquad \text{FPR} = \frac{FP}{FP + TN}
\end{equation}

The optimal threshold is then chosen as:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau} = \underset{\tau}{\text{arg max}} \ J(\tau) = \underset{\tau}{\text{arg max}} \ [\text{TPR}(\tau) - \text{FPR}(\tau)]
\end{equation}

Geometrically, this corresponds to finding the point on the ROC curve that is furthest from the diagonal line of 
no-discrimination, maximizing the gap between our model's performance and random chance.

\subsection*{The Future of Deepfake Detection}
The best result we were able to achieve was an accuracy of 68.8 percent, meaning we still misidentify over 30 percent of videos. Our model did not treat a false positive or false negative differently, though one might argue that in the field of deepfake detection a false negative, falsely classifying a deepfaked video as real, to be much more harmful. Modern deepfake detection systems generally use deep learning to achieve accuracies above 99 percent on benchmarks like FF++. A lot better than ours huh? Most of these deep learning models operate directly on pixels (or a combination) rather than through predefined features like ours, which requires significantly more training data. I trained my model on 200 videos, 100 deepfakes and 100 real, and tested on 80 videos of an even split. For reference FF++ has 5000 videos total, and other datasets have far more. My model is far from effective, but I thought it was an interesting exercise in classifiers and I learned a lot!

\section{Feature Descriptions}

For every video in the dataset I extract 5 scalar features. Each feature compares a \textit{face region} to a \textit{background region} within the same frame. The face is detected with OpenCV's Haar Cascade classifier, and the bounding box is tightened by 15\% on the left and right, 10\% on the top, and 20\% on the bottom to focus on the core face and reduce hair/neck contamination. The background is the remainder of the frame after removing the (un-tightened) bounding box. Ten frames are sampled uniformly from each video, and per-frame feature values are averaged to produce a single video-level feature vector.

\subsection*{f1 — DCT Chi-Squared Divergence}

\textbf{Intuition.} FaceSwap pastes a synthetically generated face onto an unmodified background. The face region has been processed through at least one additional encode-decode cycle, so its frequency-domain content should look systematically different from the native background.

\textbf{Computation.} Both the face crop and the background region are converted to grayscale and a 2D Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) is applied to each. The DCT expresses an image patch as a sum of cosine waves at different spatial frequencies; compression artifacts show up as anomalies in the coefficient magnitudes. We then build a 50-bin normalized histogram of the DCT coefficients for each region:

\[
  P_k = \frac{\text{count}(\text{face coefficients in bin } k)}{\text{total face coefficients}}, \quad
  Q_k = \frac{\text{count}(\text{background coefficients in bin } k)}{\text{total background coefficients}}
\]

The chi-squared divergence between these two distributions is the feature:

\[
  f_1 = \chi^2(P, Q) = \sum_{k=1}^{50} \frac{(P_k - Q_k)^2}{P_k + Q_k + \varepsilon}
\]

A log transform ($\log(1 + f_1)$) is applied at training time to correct the heavy right skew of the raw values.

\subsection*{f2 — Block-DCT $\beta$-Statistic Divergence}

\textbf{Intuition.} JPEG and video codecs compress images in $8\times 8$ pixel blocks using the DCT. When a face region is re-encoded (as happens in FaceSwap), each AC coefficient position accumulates a different amount of quantization noise than the surrounding background. This changes the \textit{scale} of the coefficient distribution at each AC position in a measurable way, even on lossless (c0) source files where the original compression is preserved.

\textbf{Computation.} The face and background regions are each divided into non-overlapping $8\times8$ pixel blocks. A 2D DCT is applied to every block. For each of the 63 AC coefficient positions (the DC component at position 0 is skipped because it simply encodes average brightness), we collect the coefficient values across all blocks and compute the scale parameter:

\[
  \beta_j = \frac{\text{std}(\{c_{b,j} : b = 1,\ldots,B\})}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad j = 1, \ldots, 63
\]

This gives two 63-dimensional vectors, $\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\text{face}}$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\text{bg}}$. The feature is the mean absolute difference across all positions:

\[
  f_2 = \frac{1}{63} \sum_{j=1}^{63} \left| \beta_j^{\text{face}} - \beta_j^{\text{bg}} \right|
\]

This approach is based on Giudice et al.\ (2021)\footnote{Giudice et al., ``Fighting Deepfakes by Detecting GAN DCT Anomalies'', \textit{MDPI Journal of Imaging}, 2021.}. A log transform is applied at training time.

\subsection*{f3 — Absolute Difference of Mean Absolute DCT Coefficients}

\textbf{Intuition.} AI face synthesis often introduces a slight blurring or smoothing of the face relative to the original. In the frequency domain, blurring suppresses high-frequency coefficients, reducing the average coefficient magnitude. If the face is blurrier than the background we expect the mean absolute DCT coefficient to be lower for the face than for the background.

\textbf{Computation.} Full-image 2D DCTs are applied to the grayscale face and background patches. The mean of the absolute values of all coefficients is computed for each:

\[
  \mu_{\text{face}} = \frac{1}{MN}\sum_{u,v} |F_{\text{face}}[u,v]|, \quad
  \mu_{\text{bg}} = \frac{1}{M'N'}\sum_{u,v} |F_{\text{bg}}[u,v]|
\]

The feature is:

\[
  f_3 = |\mu_{\text{face}} - \mu_{\text{bg}}|
\]

A log transform is applied at training time.

\subsection*{f4 — Frame-to-Frame Temporal Inconsistency}

\textbf{Intuition.} FaceSwap estimates the warp for each frame independently, which introduces small jitter in the pasted face from one frame to the next that does not follow the smooth natural motion of a real face. We should therefore see higher frame-to-frame pixel variation in the face region of deepfakes than in real videos.

\textbf{Computation.} Consecutive pairs of sampled frames $(t, t+1)$ are used. Each face crop is resized to $64\times64$, converted to grayscale, and mean-normalized to remove global lighting drift:

\[
  \tilde{I}_t = \frac{I_t}{\text{mean}(I_t) + \varepsilon}
\]

The Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD) between consecutive normalized crops is computed, then averaged across all pairs:

\[
  f_4 = \frac{1}{T-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T-1} \frac{1}{64^2}\sum_{i,j}\left|\tilde{I}_t[i,j] - \tilde{I}_{t+1}[i,j]\right|
\]

where $T = 10$ is the number of sampled frames. No log transform is applied to $f_4$.

\subsection*{f5 — Laplacian Variance Ratio}

\textbf{Intuition.} FaceSwap applies a blending and sharpening step when compositing the generated face onto the background frame. This introduces ringing and edge-enhancement artifacts in the face region that raise its apparent sharpness above that of the surrounding (unmodified) background. The Laplacian filter is the standard tool for measuring image sharpness, since it amplifies rapid intensity changes (edges, texture, ringing).

\textbf{Computation.} The discrete Laplacian operator $\nabla^2$ is applied to the grayscale face and background:

\[
  \nabla^2 f[i,j] = f[i+1,j] + f[i-1,j] + f[i,j+1] + f[i,j-1] - 4f[i,j]
\]

The feature is the ratio of their variances:

\[
  f_5 = \frac{\text{Var}(\nabla^2 I_{\text{face}})}{\text{Var}(\nabla^2 I_{\text{bg}}) + \varepsilon}
\]

Higher $f_5$ means the face is sharper (or more artifact-laden) relative to the background. A log transform is applied at training time. $f_5$ was by far the strongest individual discriminator, achieving the best standalone AUC (0.683) of all five features.
